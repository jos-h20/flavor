Shipping SyncFit — what I learned building my first App Store app

The gap between a working prototype and an App Store release is wider than it looks. Here's what surprised me most along the way.

I've been building software professionally for over a decade, but shipping a native iOS app to the App Store was a genuinely different experience. The constraints are stricter, the feedback loops are longer, and the stakes feel oddly higher — even when you're the only person on the team.

The prototype trap

SyncFit started as a weekend experiment: could I pull workout data from Apple Health and sync it somewhere useful? The prototype worked in two hours. I figured it would take a couple of weeks to polish up and ship.

It took four months.

"The last 20% of a product takes 80% of the time. But the App Store review adds a whole extra layer of unknown."

App Store review is its own discipline

Apple's review process is thorough and occasionally opaque. My first submission was rejected for a metadata issue I hadn't anticipated — the description was flagged for mentioning features that weren't visually demonstrated in the screenshots.

This sent me back to redesign the screenshot set entirely, which turned out to be worthwhile. The new screenshots are clearer and convert better. But it added three weeks to the timeline.

What I'd do differently

Prepare the App Store assets — screenshots, descriptions, keywords — before you finish development, not after. They take longer than you think, and they'll reveal UX issues in your own app that you missed during testing.
